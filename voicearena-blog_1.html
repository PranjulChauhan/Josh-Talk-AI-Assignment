The Language Tax:
How TTS Models Perform in English —
Then Fall Apart Everywhere Else

We ran 39,248 blind pairwise evaluations across five languages and eight voice AI models. The results reveal a pattern that single-language leaderboards cannot show: TTS model rankings change significantly — and sometimes dramatically — when you move beyond English. For some models, it is not a slide. It is a collapse.

Most TTS benchmarks publish one leaderboard. One ranking. As if voice quality is a property that holds constant regardless of language, script, or phonetic system. As if the model that speaks the most natural English will also speak the most natural Japanese, Arabic, or Hindi.

It does not.

Over April and May 2026, VoiceArena ran 39,248 blind pairwise evaluations across five languages: English (en-US), Hindi (hi-IN), Arabic (ar-MSA), Japanese (ja-JP), and Brazilian Portuguese (pt-BR). Trained annotators with native or near-native proficiency in each language listened to two models reading the same sentence and chose which sounded better. In 19,178 of those evaluations, they also wrote why — producing 32,148 individual written comments explaining specific failures or strengths.

What we found is not a small deviation from the English ranking. For several models, it is a reversal.

Central Finding

TTS model rankings shift significantly across languages. A model winning 40.8% of English comparisons can reach 63.0% in Japanese. A model at 24.3% in English can fall to 8.0% in Japanese. A model finishing last in English can rank third in Hindi. A single-language leaderboard obscures all of this.

What Makes VoiceArena's Evaluation Different

The dominant TTS benchmarks — including Artificial Analysis's leaderboard and most published MOS studies — evaluate models in English, or in one language at a time, using automated metrics or crowdsourced ratings at scale. These approaches are fast and reproducible. They miss two things.

First, they evaluate a single language. A developer deploying TTS for a Hindi-language banking app, an Arabic customer support agent, or a Japanese audiobook platform gets no direct signal from an English leaderboard. They are forced to extrapolate across a dimension that, as this evaluation shows, does not extrapolate reliably.

Second, automated metrics and casual crowdworkers do not catch what trained native-language evaluators catch. A phoneme error rate does not distinguish between "uh" correctly realized in English and "uh" phonetically wrong in Arabic. A native Arabic annotator writing a structured comment does. A word error rate metric might partially credit a Japanese kanji misreading. A native Japanese speaker hears it as a wrong word — and documents exactly which character was misread and how.

VoiceArena is, to our knowledge, the only platform running multilingual blind pairwise TTS evaluation with trained native-language annotators at this scale. The combination of five languages, structured written comments, and pairwise methodology — rather than absolute scoring — produces a human preference signal that reflects what real listeners in each language actually hear.

Blind pairwise evaluation — the same approach used by Chatbot Arena for large language models — is more reliable than absolute MOS scoring because it removes individual calibration differences. When you ask someone to rate a voice 1–5, everyone uses the scale differently. When you ask "which of these two is better," the answer is more consistent, and aggregating thousands of these comparisons via Elo produces a stable preference ranking.

Finding 1 — Google's Advantage Grows With Language Distance From English

Google DeepMind Gemini 3.1 Flash TTS wins 40.8% of English comparisons — competitive, but not dominant. In Japanese, its win rate climbs to 63.0% across 2,189 comparisons. In Arabic, 61.5% across 2,695. In Hindi, 52.2%. In Portuguese, 49.5%.

The pattern is consistent: Google's relative advantage increases as structural distance from English increases. Japanese and Arabic — the two languages most phonetically and prosodically distinct from English in this evaluation — show Google at its most dominant. The evaluator comments point to two underlying strengths: prosody that adapts to language-specific intonation patterns, and pronunciation accuracy on language-specific vocabulary including proper nouns, technical terms, and character-level readings in Japanese.

Google's customer support performance is particularly strong: 56.5% win rate across 4,089 CS comparisons — the largest single use-case category in this dataset and the one with the highest practical stakes for deployment.

Finding 2 — The Japanese Collapse: Microsoft Azure at 8.0%

Microsoft Azure Dragon HD Omni performs at 24.3% in English — weak but present. In Japanese, across 2,199 comparisons, it reaches 8.0%. Human evaluators preferred a different model 92% of the time.

The evaluator comments are specific. Japanese annotators flagged three categories of failure: kanji misreadings (wrong phonetic readings of written characters), flat pitch-accent (Japanese is a pitch-accent language; English prosodic patterns imported into Japanese speech are immediately detectable as unnatural), and technical vocabulary errors including chemical formulas and proper names.

Finding 3 — The Reversal: Cartesia Sonic-3 From Last to Third

Cartesia Sonic-3 finishes last in English at 13.5% across 1,784 comparisons. In Hindi, across 2,475 comparisons, it reaches 34.1% — third place behind only Google and ElevenLabs. In Portuguese, 31.1%, also ahead of three other models.

This is the most direct evidence that English performance does not predict multilingual performance. A team selecting TTS models for a Hindi or Portuguese product using only an English leaderboard would eliminate Cartesia entirely — and miss the third-best performing model for their use case.

For any team deploying TTS in a non-English language: English benchmark rankings are baseline signal, not deployment guidance. The Cartesia reversal is not an edge case. It is a demonstration that the relationship between English and non-English performance is weak enough to be operationally misleading.

Finding 4 — "Robotic" Is the Primary Failure Mode Across Every Language

We categorized 32,148 individual written evaluator comments by failure type. One category dominates: descriptions of voice as robotic, unnatural, or mechanical. This appeared in 3,130 comments and was directly associated with 1,926 model losses — more than any other failure category by a significant margin.

Implication

Sounding natural is not an aesthetic preference. It is the primary decision axis for human evaluators — consistently, across five different languages and cultures. Reducing robotic voice is the single highest-leverage improvement available to most models in this evaluation. Any model that closes its naturalness gap will see proportional improvement in human preference comparisons.

Language-Specific Failure Profiles

Each language surfaces failure patterns specific to its phonetic, prosodic, and grammatical structure. The written comments — produced by trained native-language annotators — make these patterns precise rather than impressionistic.

Japanese (ja-JP) — Kanji Reading and Pitch Accent

Japanese requires a lookup system that maps written characters to their correct phonetic readings in context. Multiple readings exist for many kanji; the correct choice depends on surrounding words and meaning. Models without robust Japanese G2P coverage produce systematic misreadings that native speakers detect immediately. Annotators also consistently flagged flat prosody — the absence of pitch-accent patterns that are phonemically meaningful in Japanese and have no English equivalent.

Arabic (ar-MSA) — Numbers, Filler Words, Hallucination

The most common Arabic-specific failures: numeric misreading (tracking IDs, currency amounts, decimal values read incorrectly), mispronounced filler words (Arabic filler sounds carry different phonetic realizations than English equivalents), and hallucination — words added to or removed from the output text. Hallucination is a critical failure in customer support contexts where verbatim accuracy is functionally required.

Hindi (hi-IN) — Does Language Specialization Beat Scale?

The Hindi evaluation includes Sarvam AI Bulbul-V3, a model built specifically for Indian languages. Its performance is a natural test of whether specialization compensates for scale. Sarvam outperforms xAI Grok (23.7%), Microsoft Azure (19.4%), and OpenAI GPT-4o-mini (17.6%) — but trails Google (52.2%), ElevenLabs (45.1%), and Cartesia (34.1%). Specialization is a real advantage. At current scale, it is not an absolute one.

Implications for Model Builders

1. Language-specific G2P coverage gaps are the proximate cause of most non-English pronunciation failures. The Japanese kanji misreadings in this dataset are systematic, not random. They are documentable, measurable, and correctable through targeted training data expansion.

2. Prosody modeling must be language-specific. Japanese is a pitch-accent language. Arabic has phonemic length distinctions. Hindi has retroflex consonants. Models trained primarily on English import English prosodic structure into other languages — annotators across all three describe the result as flat, robotic, or foreign-sounding.

3. Number and ID reading is a high-frequency, high-impact, tractable failure. It should be treated as a first-class evaluation criterion for any team targeting customer support deployment — the largest use case in this dataset.

4. Reducing robotic voice is the highest-leverage improvement for most models. The naturalness gap between the top two models (Google, ElevenLabs, both at ~2% robotic citation rate) and the bottom two (OpenAI, Microsoft, both above 9%) is large, consistent, and strongly correlated with overall win rates.

5. English benchmarks should not drive non-English deployment decisions. Cartesia's 2.5× reversal from English to Hindi is not a statistical outlier. It is a demonstration that the relationship between English and non-English human preference is too weak to extrapolate from reliably.

Conclusion

The voice AI industry benchmarks models in English. It deploys models everywhere. This evaluation documents the cost of that gap.

39,248 pairwise comparisons across five languages, with 32,148 written explanations from trained native-language evaluators, produce a picture that no English-only leaderboard can show. Google leads across four of five languages. ElevenLabs is the consistent second. Microsoft reaches 8.0% in Japanese. Cartesia, last in English, ranks third in Hindi.

The robotic voice problem is the most widespread unsolved challenge in this evaluation. The number-reading problem is documented and tractable. The prosody gap in non-English languages is real, language-specific, and waiting to be closed.

A single-language leaderboard tells you how a model performs in English. It tells you almost nothing about how it performs for the majority of the world's speakers. That is what this evaluation is designed to change.
